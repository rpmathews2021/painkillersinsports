The Effects of Painkilers in Professional Sports

 In professional sports injuries are very common, whether it is in practice or in games.  But some professional leagues like the NFL have a problem with painkillers.  These drugs are prescribed to help with the pain from injuries.  Not only can these drugs be potentially deadly they also can lead to life long addictions that could ruin lives.  In this analysis i will be talking most about the NFL because of the availability of the information and the stories from the athletes themselves.  

 

 

Positives of Painkillers:

 Although there are not many positives there are a few.  Painkillers are very effective.  Painkillers almost seem neccisary in a sport like american football when you step on the field there is a 4.1% chance of getting injuried.(ProFootballLogic)  Most painkillers cut off the brains access to the feeling of pain several different ways creating this feeling of no pain.(Webmd)  These medicines are very effective, which might seem pretty good in you have just torn your acl.  But other than that there are no other real positves about painkillers. 

 

 

Negatives of painkillers:

Painkillers are extremely addictive, several stories about players taking these drugs have surfaced.  Take Kyle Turley for example who recently came out about his problem with the medications in an interview with vice sports.  He talks about how they effected his attitude and how they negatively impacted his life.  He talks about one time having stockpiles of painkillers because of their availability to him.  These drugs shouldnt be as available as they are. Recently the NFL cam out with a statement that in the calendar year 2012 "the average team prescribed nearly 5,777 doses of nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs and 2,213 doses of controlled medications to its players, according to a March 2013 internal document from Lawrence Brown, the NFL-employed medical adviser who oversees its drug issues."(Washington Post)Painkillers are also very dangerous, a very painkiller Vioxx was pulled of shelves on September 30, 2004 because of their connection to increased heart failure in patients.(Robinskaplin)
